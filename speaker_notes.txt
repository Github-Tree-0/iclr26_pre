=== Slide 1: Title ===
Hello everyone, I'm very happy to present our work, SCas4D.
In this talk, I'll show how introducing structure into dynamic Gaussian optimization
makes online 4D novel view synthesis significantly faster and more stable.

=== Slide 2: Online 4DGS: Fast Adaptation ===
Let me first set up the problem.
We work in the online persistent 4D novel view synthesis setting.
At each new frame, we start from the Gaussians of the previous frame
and need to quickly adapt them to match the new observations.
This is challenging for three reasons.
First, each frame inherits the state of the previous one, so errors can accumulate.
Second, motion can be large and highly non-rigid.
And third, we have a very limited iteration budget per frame,
so the optimization has to be both fast and accurate.

=== Slide 3: Problem: Optimization Instability ===
The core problem is that existing methods like Dynamic3DGS
update each Gaussian largely independently.
Under a small per-frame iteration budget, this leads to unstable optimization —
individual Gaussians can drift in inconsistent directions, or the structure can collapse entirely.
As you can see here, the baseline produces blurry or broken results,
while our method stays close to the ground truth.
This problem gets especially severe for large motion and non-rigid deformation.

=== Slide 4: Key Insight: Structured Deformation (step 1) ===
So what's the key insight?
The observation is that real-world deformation is not arbitrary.
Nearby Gaussians tend to move together —
a robot arm moves as a whole, a piece of cloth folds coherently.
This means we don't need to treat every Gaussian independently.
Instead, we can model motion in a structured way,
handling large-scale movement first,
and then refining local details afterward.

=== Slide 5: Key Insight: Structured Deformation (step 2) ===
Concretely, we propose to organize Gaussians into groups
and move them in a coarse-to-fine manner.
As shown in the figure, instead of independent per-point updates on the left,
we get hierarchical group-level motion on the right.
This structure makes optimization much more stable,
because each group of Gaussians is constrained to move coherently.

=== Slide 6: Method: SCas4D ===
This idea leads to our method, SCas4D.
Starting from the Gaussians of the previous frame,
we organize them into a K-layer cluster hierarchy.
Each layer defines a deformation function,
and the full scene deformation is the composition of all layers from coarse to fine —
D1 composed with D2, all the way to Dk.
The whole model is supervised end-to-end through the rendered image loss.

=== Slide 7: Cluster Deformation ===
For each cluster, we model three components:
rotation, translation, and position-dependent scaling.
This parameterization is interpretable and expressive enough
to capture the major motion patterns in dynamic scenes,
while being much easier to optimize than unconstrained per-point motion.

=== Slide 8: Coarse-to-Fine Hierarchy ===
An important property of our hierarchy is that
we don't sacrifice fine-grained modeling power for the sake of structure.
The coarse layers quickly align large-scale structures across frames,
the finer layers handle smaller local motion,
and per-Gaussian residuals on top preserve fine details.
So the hierarchy speeds up convergence without over-constraining the representation.

=== Slide 9: End-to-End via Rendering ===
The full pipeline is straightforward.
We deform the Gaussians using the cascaded cluster hierarchy,
render the deformed scene, and backpropagate through the image loss.
SCas4D fits naturally into the existing online 4DGS framework —
the only change is replacing independent per-Gaussian updates
with our structured cascaded deformation.

=== Slide 16: Better Tracking ===
Beyond rendering quality, our stable deformation field also benefits downstream tasks.
Because the method produces more coherent per-Gaussian motion,
it can be directly used for dense point tracking.
As shown here, our tracking trajectories in blue are much closer to the ground truth in red,
and the visualization on the right shows clean, consistent motion paths across the sequence.

=== Slide 17: Self-supervised Segmentation ===
Another nice byproduct of our structured deformation is self-supervised segmentation.
By clustering the learned motion parameters over time,
we can automatically recover motion-consistent parts of the scene —
without any semantic labels.
On the left is the input video, and on the right you can see
that our method correctly separates the moving boxes into distinct segments.

=== Slide 18: Takeaway ===
To summarize, SCas4D replaces independent per-Gaussian updates
with structured cascaded deformation.
This simple change gives faster convergence under a low iteration budget,
more stable rendering quality,
and useful motion cues for both tracking and segmentation.
If you're interested in more details, please check out our paper.
Thank you very much.
